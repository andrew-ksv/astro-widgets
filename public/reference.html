Auroras

Auroras, also known as polar lights, are natural light displays predominantly seen in high-latitude regions near the Arctic and Antarctic. In the Northern Hemisphere, they are often called Aurora Borealis (Northern Lights), while in the Southern Hemisphere they are referred to as Aurora Australis (Southern Lights).

Auroras occur when charged particles from the Sun, mainly electrons and protons, collide with Earth's magnetic field and upper atmosphere. These particles excite the gases in the atmosphere - mostly oxygen and nitrogen - which then emit visible light. The colors of the aurora depend on which gases are involved and their altitude:

  • ðŸ”· Purple and blue â€“ nitrogen at lower altitudes.

The Planetary K-index directly affects the visibility of auroras. When geomagnetic activity is low, auroras are faint and usually limited to polar regions. As geomagnetic activity increases, auroras become brighter, more dynamic, and can be observed farther from the poles. During strong geomagnetic storms (K-index 5-9), auroras may be visible at much lower latitudes than usual.
